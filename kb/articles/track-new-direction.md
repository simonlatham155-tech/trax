## BBC Radio 1

Judge Jules Show — October 1998 (3rd, 9th and 16th). Occasionally credited on air as "Soundstation" (typo).

## The Direct Line from the GusGus Office

In 1997 Simon was at the GusGus office in Reykjavik when the DAT of Sasha's remix of *The Light* arrived. His impression on first listen: a gated vocal, progressive by the day's standards but leaning toward trance. The club sound was shifting.

*New Direction* — the Lunn & Latham mix — was the most vocal-led trance track Soundsation had made. It came the year after that room. Hearing the direction in 1997 and making a record in that direction in 1998 is the pattern running through the whole career: being inside the movement before the wider market confirmed it.

## Ahead of the Genre

*New Direction* was a full lead vocal trance record released in 1998. That matters because vocal trance as a recognised, commercially dominant form didn't arrive until 1999–2001. ATB's *9 PM (Till I Come)* — widely cited as a turning point — came in 1998 but broke through in 1999. Alice Deejay's *Better Off Alone*, Darude's *Sandstorm*, the wave of Radio 1-crossover vocal trance records — all of that was still ahead.

Soundsation was producing and releasing a fully formed vocal trance record while the genre's infrastructure was still being built. There were no dedicated trance radio shows at scale, no *A State of Trance* (that launched in 2001), no superclub nights specifically branded around trance. The record circulated on vinyl through DJ networks into a scene that didn't yet have a complete name for what it was hearing.

## Judge Jules and the Pipeline

Judge Jules was one of the few BBC Radio 1 DJs actively programming harder and more melodic trance in 1998. His show was the radio home for the end of the spectrum that Pete Tong's Essential Selection didn't fully cover — faster, more euphoric, more explicitly uplifting. Three plays in October 1998 in a single month indicates genuine enthusiasm.

The fact that a full lead vocal trance record from an independent UK imprint could receive this level of Radio 1 support in 1998 — before the genre's commercial peak — confirms that the pipeline existed before the mainstream arrived to fill it.

## The Significance of the Timing

The standard account of vocal trance places its emergence around 1999–2001. *New Direction* in 1998 predates that by at least a year. It is evidence that the sound was being developed by artists working in progressive house and early trance before the genre had achieved any commercial scale — that the musical idea arrived before the industry caught up with it.

This is the pattern the entire Soundsation catalogue documents: *Peace & Joy* in the white-label era before FFRR; *Do You Feel It?* circulating through the DJ network before any release; *New Direction* in vocal trance before vocal trance. The work consistently preceded the wider scene's arrival at the same point.

## The Title

*New Direction* is named appropriately. The move from Stress Records progressive house to SOS Recordings trance was deliberate and reflected the direction the scene was moving — but the scale of that move, and its timing, placed the record outside the infrastructure that would later support the genre. It was the right direction, early.

## SOS Recordings

Soundsation's own imprint, distributed via Amato (1994–1997) and MOS Music (1997–1999). The choice to self-release on SOS rather than through an established trance label reflects both the constraints of the time — dedicated trance labels at scale barely existed in 1998 in the UK — and the project's history of operating outside major-label structures following the *Peace & Joy* experience.
