## The Received Account

The standard history of vocal trance places its emergence at 1999–2001. ATB's *9 PM (Till I Come)* breaking into the mainstream in 1999. Alice Deejay's *Better Off Alone* the same year. Darude's *Sandstorm* in 2000. These are the records cited as the beginning.

## What Was Actually Happening Earlier

The records that broke through commercially were not the first of their kind. Artists working in the UK progressive house and early trance scenes were producing full lead vocal trance records before the genre had a name, before the superclub trance nights existed, before *A State of Trance* (2001) gave it a weekly global platform.

Soundsation's *New Direction* (SOS Recordings, 1998) is a documented example: a full lead vocal trance record that received multiple BBC Radio 1 plays via Judge Jules in October 1998 — a full year before the genre's commonly cited commercial arrival.

## Why the Gap Exists

The records that preceded the commercial wave circulated on vinyl through DJ networks. Without streaming, without large-scale trance radio infrastructure, without the compilation series and podcast culture that would later document the genre, they left a smaller trace. The commercial breakthrough records had marketing budgets, major label distribution, and the infrastructure of a genre that had just arrived. The early ones did not.

This creates a false impression that the sound was invented by the artists who broke it commercially. In practice, the musical idea was being developed years earlier by producers working in the space between progressive house and the trance that was emerging — hearing where things were going and making records there before the audience had arrived.

## The Progressive House Connection

This pattern mirrors the broader relationship between progressive house and trance. The sound that would become mainstream trance was not invented by the artists who popularised it — it was developed in the progressive house underground, in studios and on vinyl, by producers who were following the music where it was going rather than following the market.

*New Direction* documents this from the inside: a project that had been on Stress Records and the Renaissance compilation in 1996, moving to full lead vocal trance on its own label in 1998. Not a pivot toward a trend — the trend hadn't happened yet. A movement toward where the music was going.

## The Infrastructure Problem

In 1998, the UK trance market barely existed as a distinct commercial entity. There were no dedicated trance labels operating at scale. The major trance compilation series were in their infancy. BBC Radio 1 had Judge Jules as a trance-adjacent programmer, but no dedicated trance show. *A State of Trance* was three years away.

A full lead vocal trance record in 1998 had nowhere obvious to go. The DJ network could carry it — and Judge Jules' three plays in a single month confirm it did — but the wider commercial infrastructure that would have amplified it simply wasn't there yet.

## What It Tells Us

The history of any genre is usually written backward from its commercial breakthrough. The artists who arrive early, who document the sound before it has an audience, tend to disappear from that account. The records exist — on vinyl, in DJ archives, in chart entries and tracklists — but the narrative gravitates toward the moment of mass arrival.

The early vocal trance records from 1997–1999 are evidence that the sound preceded the scene that claimed to have created it.
